Part 1: Mirror and Glass Materials

  • Show a sequence of six images of scene CBspheres.dae rendered with max_ray_depth set to 0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, and 100. The other settings should be at least 64 samples per pixel and 4 samples per light.

Max Ray Depth 0

Max Ray Depth 1

At a max ray depth of 0, we only see the light source itself and the light rays it directly sends towards the camera as we do not account for any bounces of light yet.

Increasing the max ray depth 1, we can now see objects directly illuminated by the light source including the light on the tops of the spheres that reflect towards the camera. Overall, we can see much of the inside of the Cornell box by allowing rays up to depth 1.

Max Ray Depth 2

Max Ray Depth 3

At a max ray depth of 2, the mirror qualities of the two spheres respectively start to become evident. For the purely mirror sphere on the left, we can see most of the scene reflected because we can now consider light rays that bounce off the environment (such as the walls) and onto the mirror surface and towards the camera. The glass sphere on the right starts to become visible but is still relatively dark because we are unable to fully consider light rays refracting through the internals of this sphere when limited to a max ray depth of 2.

Additionally, with 2 bounces, the ceiling is also now illuminated because the ceiling is illuminated by the light rays that bounce back up from the other elements of the scene such as the walls and floor, which require at least a max ray depth of 2 to be accounted for since it already takes one bounce to hit the directly illuminated elements.

Increasing the max ray depth to 3 greatly improves the image’s lighting accuracy. The ceiling is now reflected by the mirror sphere on the left, and the glass sphere is far brighter now that light rays going through the inside of this sphere are accounted for. The inclusion of these internal refracted rays at a max ray depth of 3 also explains the bright spot we see under the sphere where the light that passes through the sphere and ultimately exits is refracted. Essentially, we see the ground below the glass sphere also illuminated now because with 3 bounces, we can account for rays that enter the sphere, exit the sphere, and then hit the ground.

Though more subtle, the overall lighting in the scene also becomes a bit brighter with more bounces of light illuminating the scene.

Max Ray Depth 4

Max Ray Depth 5

At a ray depth of 4, we finally get a complete render with no major missing elements. The glass sphere now appears properly and clearly in the reflection on the mirror sphere’s surface because the light rays that pass through the glass sphere can now also continue on to bounce off other elements in the scene after exiting the glass sphere. The mirror surface of the mirror sphere is one such element, which leads to the appearance of the glass ball’s reflection.

Also, a refracted spot of light appears on the purple wall now that we consider light rays with 4 bounces because light can now exit the glass sphere, bounce off the floor, and then also hit the purple wall before arriving at the camera.

Though subtle, we can also see a reflection of light on the bottom of the glass sphere that makes this region appear brighter than before with light bouncing from the box back onto the face of the glass sphere with the 4th bounce. Finally, the front of the glass sphere also appears brighter with more light bouncing back onto the sphere overall.

Increasing the maximum ray depth from 4 to 5, we see no meaningful changes for the first time.

Max Ray Depth 100

Again, going from a max depth of 5 to 100 yields little appreciable gains in lighting fidelity. The one thing we noticed in increasing the max ray depth up to 100 is that the reflection on the bottom of the sphere appears ever so slightly smoother and less grainy than before.

Part 4: Depth of Field

In a few sentences, explain the differences between a pinhole camera model and a thin-lens camera model.

  • In a pinhole camera model, we assume that everything is perfectly in focus. Of course, this is not a realistic model at all — actual cameras (including our eyes themselves) can be modeled as having focal distances and finite apertures. The thin-lens camera model, as the name suggests, reformulates our model to have a finite aperture and a given focal length, with a given object only being in focus if it lies within the focal plane (at z = -focalDistance).

Show a "focus stack" where you focus at 4 visibly different depths through a scene.
