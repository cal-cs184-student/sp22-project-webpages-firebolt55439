Thus, our algorithm can be no worse than checking each sample within the bounding box of a triangle.

Task 2

  • Walk through your supersampling algorithm and data structures. Why is supersampling useful? What modifications did you make to the rasterization pipeline in the process? Explain how you used supersampling to antialias your triangles.
  • Our supersampling algorithm revolves around 2 key changes. First, we modified all rasterize_* functions to draw into the sample buffer rather than the outputted frame buffer. Second, before the output image is rendered, we downsample the sample buffer to fit into the output frame buffer by using a box filter (aka taking the average of all mapped pixel values in the input sample buffer to a given pixel in the output frame buffer). The changes made to the rasterization pipeline were modifying all the rasterize functions to write into the sample buffer and implementing the box filter / coalescing operation to resolve the frame buffer at the end.
  • Supersampling is useful because it smoothes out “jaggies” and the Moire pattern in images, which appear in some test images (especially those involving straight lines or circles). These aliasing artifacts naturally occur due to the implications of sampling at an insufficient frequency over high-frequency regions of the image — supersampling ameliorates this issue by essentially increasing the sampling frequency everywhere.

  • Barycentric coordinates involve constructing a 2D coordinate system that uses the three triangle vertices as elementary basis vectors. By projecting the Cartesian coordinates into the Barycentric coordinate system, it provides smooth linear interpolation in the space contained within the triangle as a triple of weights that represent the interpolated “portion” of each vertex at a point. Task 5

  • Explain pixel sampling in your own words and describe how you implemented it to perform texture mapping. Briefly discuss the two different pixel sampling methods, nearest and bilinear.
  • Pixel sampling is the process of mapping textures to points in the screen space from the texture space in a texture image. By sampling the corresponding points in texture space, textures can be assigned to points in the screen space. Pixel sampling was implemented with 2 options: nearest-pixel sampling and bilinear sampling. To first determine the corresponding pixel in texture space for each pixel in the screen space, we used barycentric coordinates since the 3 weights (alpha, beta, and gamma) found in screen space remained valid in texture space. Using these weights for each sampled point in the screen space, we applied them to the texture space coordinates corresponding to the vertices of a given triangle to determine the corresponding texture space coordinates. For nearest-pixel sampling, we used this texture space coordinate and then rounded its u and v values to find the nearest texel whose color is then assigned to the screen space coordinate. As for bilinear sampling, we calculated the nearest 4 texels from the barycentric coordinate derived texture space point. The 4 texels that we applied bilinear interpolation to are determined by simply offsetting the indices of the central point to obtain 4 points along each of the 4 main diagonals from the central point. The color data from each of these texels is interpolated and assigned to the screen space point.

  • At both sampling rates, the bilinear pixel sampling option produces smoother lines with fewer artifacts than nearest pixel sampling, which has a much more “discrete” pixel appearance. While nearest pixel sampling combined with an increased sampling rate of 16 does yield gains in image fidelity and antialiasing power, it still lags behind bilinear sampling even when paired with a sample rate of 1. In general, there will be large differences between these two sampling methods when there is a high level of detail in a concentrated area within an image. This behavior arises because the more simplistic process of nearest pixel sampling is not as effective at capturing the large amount of details and textures in a given image region, since it samples and utilizes less of the texture information around a given pixel, and will therefore not be able to perform as well in producing a representative visual result.

Task 6

  • Explain level sampling in your own words and describe how you implemented it for texture mapping.
  • Level sampling is the process of sampling textures from the corresponding mipmap texture level for a given pixel. The level is determined based on the distance of the given pixel to the two immediately neighboring pixels above and to the right in texture space. Depending on the level sampling technique, these two distances are used differently. However, these distances, in general, are used as a measure of the frequency of the texture around a given pixel, which in turn can be used in calculations to determine the best corresponding level to use. In the nearest level sampling we implemented, we took the log of the maximum of the two distances and rounded the result to determine the level to use for texture mapping at a given pixel. From here, we simply assigned the color contained at the corresponding location in texture space to the pixel in screen space. We also implemented bilinear level sampling where we computed the mipmap level as a continuous number and then computed a weighted sum using one texture sample from each of the adjacent mipmap levels (determined using the round and ceil operators) using linear interpolation.
  • You can now adjust your sampling technique by selecting pixel sampling, level sampling, or the number of samples per pixel. Describe the tradeoffs between speed, memory usage, and antialiasing power between the three various techniques.
  • Adjusting the number of samples per pixel (super sampling) can achieve good visual results and effectively antialias, but there are significant costs associated with this technique. With higher super sampling rates such as 16 samples per pixel, the visual results can be good with high antialiasing power. However, increasing the number of samples per pixel quickly becomes very expensive in terms of both computation/speed and also memory usage. Increasing the sampling rate by a factor of A leads to the need to perform A times more computation per pixel for a total of A * width * height where width and height refer to the size of the image in screen space. The amount of necessary computation blows up quickly as the sample rate increases. Additionally, increasing the sample rate also requires using more memory to store the higher resolution image that will be downsampled to produce the final resulting image.
  • Compared to super sampling, level sampling is much more memory efficient requiring only ⅓ more memory than just storing a single full resolution texture image. The computation required to implement this technique differs depending on the specific technique used to determine the level(s) to use. However, unlike with super sampling, there isn’t a concern of this figure blowing up much since the number of computations required per pixel is fixed for a given implementation choice and doesn’t require progressively increasing the number of computations to achieve better results. Although the memory overhead is relatively minimal, one consideration and drawback might be that we may have to make many non-contiguous memory accesses as we map textures to each pixel since we have to jump between accessing the sample buffer and the different mipmaps. Examining the memory accesses of super sampling, the accesses can be made more contiguous since we just have to iterate through the sample buffer row by row sequentially in each part of the implementation, which would confer better cache locality. That said, level sampling is overall a cheaper method to achieve good antialiased results at much lower levels of computation and storage overhead than super sampling.
  • Finally, for pixel sampling, the costs are similar to that of level sampling, but it skips the step of having to calculate the mipmap level for a given pixel and when used on its own just samples from a single texture image with varying amounts of computation needed to determine the resulting texture with the options in this project being the very fast and low cost nearest pixel sampling and the more expensive but more effective bilinear pixel sampling technique. Similar to level sampling, the amount of computation per pixel is fixed for a given technique and relatively low cost for the quality of antialiasing it can yield. As for memory considerations, since this technique doesn’t require additional mipmaps or up-sampled images, it doesn’t require more memory beyond a single texture map image and the sample buffer.
