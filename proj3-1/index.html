Part 1
  • Walk through the ray generation and primitive intersection parts of the rendering pipeline.
  • Ray generation is part of the ray tracing step that helps to map coordinates from an image to world space and return a corresponding ray originating from the camera to the mapped point in world space. As a part of this ray tracing process, the pipeline also needs to determine the radiance along these rays, which requires determining a ray’s intersection with primitives and the radiance that results from these intersections.
  • Explain the triangle intersection algorithm you implemented in your own words.
  • We implemented the Möller-Trombore algorithm as a helper function to calculate ray-triangle intersections. This algorithm helped us efficiently compute the solution to the ray-triangle intersection equation for linear equations of the form Mx = b. The algorithm itself is derived from a straightforward application of Cramer’s Rule to the corresponding linear system of equations representing the intersection problem. In using this algorithm, we obtained the value t — the time the ray intersected the triangle — along with the barycentric coordinate coefficients of the intersection point relative to the triangle. With those barycentric coordinates, we were able to compute the normal vector as a weighted average of the three normal vectors of the triangle vertices (using barycentric coordinates of each vertex as its weight).

Part 2
  • Walk through your BVH construction algorithm. Explain the heuristic you chose for picking the splitting point.
  • Our BVH construction algorithm recursively splits a given bounding box until leaf nodes are produced such that they contain less than a constant number of primitives. At each recursive step, we start by creating a bounding box containing all of the given primitives. From there, we evaluate our base case on whether the newly generated bounding box constitutes a leaf node based on how many primitives are contained in it. If we have arrived at a leaf node, we create a new node object, assign its relevant attributes, and return it. Otherwise, our algorithm continues on to split the current bounding box in 2.
  • The heuristic we chose for picking the splitting point is to calculate the centroid value along the longest axis spanned by a given bounding box and using that value as the split point. In the case where all primitives are on one side of a split, we manually adjust the split point by 1 primitive to ensure both sides have at least one primitive.
  • After determining this split point, we create a new node and assign its left and right children attributes to recursive calls to construct_bvh(...), passing in the corresponding range of primitive pointers to each call. Specifically, the calculated split point is used to partition the set of given primitive pointers, and the pointer determined to be the split pointer becomes the end of the set of the pointers passed to the left child and the starting pointer of the set passed to the right child. The algorithm concludes when all recursive calls are finished.

Part 3
  • Walk through both implementations of the direct lighting function.
  • We implemented two implementations of the direct lighting function: estimate_direct_lighting_hemisphere(...) and estimate_direct_lighting_importance(...) that estimate the direct lighting on a point in different ways. In both implementations, we are estimating the irradiance at a point that a ray from our camera intersects a scene object. We call this point of interest the hit point.  
  1. estimate_direct_lighting_hemisphere(...) samples inverse rays arriving at our point of interest from uniformly across a hemisphere.
  1. Using a Monte Carlo estimator, this implementation estimates the total light arriving in a hemisphere around the point of interest.
  2. For each of x samples taken at a given hit point, we sample a random outgoing direction into the hemisphere from this point and determine the light level at the point this ray intersects in the scene. We then normalize this light value by the pdf of hemisphere directions and add this value to a variable tracking the total light along all of the sampled directions.
  3. Finally, we average the sum of all the light values from the samples and return the average as our estimate of the irradiance at the hit point.
  1. estimate_direct_lighting_importance(...) also estimates the light arriving at a hit point, but this implementation only samples from lights in a scene as opposed to uniformly in a hemisphere.
  1. At each hit point, we importance sample all of the lights in the scene. If a given light is a point light, we only take one sample for that light since all samples would be the same. Otherwise, this implementation takes x samples for each non-point light source in the scene that is in front of the surface at the hit point.
  2. As with the uniform sampling implementation, if a casted ray from the hit point intersects the scene before arriving at the light source, it’s determined that the hit point is in the shadow of the scene in that sampled direction.
  3. As with the uniform hemisphere sampling implementation, we also use the reflectance equation to determine the total amount of outgoing light from a given source for sample rays that arrive at light sources.
  4. For each light source, we normalize our samples by the pdf of outgoing directions towards a light, and average the sum of the sampled light values. These average light values for each light are summed together and returned as our overall estimate of the light incident to the hit point.

Part 4
  • Walk through your implementation of the indirect lighting function.
  • In our PathTracer::at_least_one_bounce_radiance (the indirect lighting function), we first terminate our recursion if ray depth <= 1 or if a coin flip with probability 35% is won (Roulette termination). Otherwise, we set up our coordinate space like normal, and compute the one-bounce radiance (which does lighting estimation either by uniform hemisphere sampling or importance sampling) as a result of the ray. We then call sample_f to sample an incident light direction and its pdf, which we then cast out to find the next intersection in time interval [EPS_F, isect.t - EPS_F]. If the casted ray hits something, we recursively call ourselves and multiply by the standard Monte Carlo and light equation factors (twice cosine angle of the vector times the sample_f output, divided by the pdf and 100% - 35% to account for Roulette).

Part 5
  • Walk through your implementation of adaptive sampling.
  • In our implementation of adaptive sampling, we keep track of the running sum of illuminances and the running sum of the squared illuminances, which we can use to efficiently compute the average and variance (and thereby standard deviation) on-the-fly. The core of our code from Part 1 is unchanged, except the ray’s depth is set to max_ray_depth, and every samplesPerBatch samples, we check if we are within a 95% confidence interval of the true pixel value, as measured by a 2-tailed z-test with 95% confidence (computing I = 1.96 * std dev / sqrt(# samples) and comparing it to 5% * mean). If we are within that confidence interval, then no need to take further samples, so we stop early and record how many samples we actually ended up taking in the sampleCountBuffer.
