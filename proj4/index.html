Part 5: Shaders

  • Explain in your own words what is a shader program and how vertex and fragment shaders work together to create lighting and material effects.

A shader program is an isolated, fast-executing program that runs in parallel on a GPU to enable real-time rendering at monitor framerates (60fps and faster). CPU rendering would simply not be fast enough to render at these framerates, hence the need for a program to run directly on the GPU. Vertex shaders transform vertices (e.g. translation, rotation, scaling, and the like), and write out their final positions to a special position vector. Fragment shaders complete the rendering process post-rasterization, taking in the attributes of computed fragments and outputting the computed colors. Vertex shaders are responsible for effects such as bumps in the bump displacement mapping, while fragment shaders perform the actual shading and coloring; as a result, both have their part to play in lighting and coloring effects. Material effects, such as mirroring, would be implemented at the fragment shader level since fragment shaders are responsible for light ray simulation to compute the output colors.

  • Explain the Blinn-Phong shading model in your own words. Show a screenshot of your Blinn-Phong shader outputting only the ambient component, a screenshot only outputting the diffuse component, a screenshot only outputting the specular component, and one using the entire Blinn-Phong model.

The Blinn-Phong shading model separates lighting into three components: ambient, specular, and diffuse. Ambient lighting is due to the light omnipresent in the environment, and is the most straightforward of the three (is readily modeled as just adding light in general to the output color components). Diffuse lighting considers the light rays and their intensity (compensated for radial distance fall-off) and their dot product with the surface normal, capturing diffuse reflections on objects. Lastly, specular highlighting is derived from considering where bright light bouncing off a surface “bleeds over” to different camera perspectives (even if the light ray were not directly pointed at you but to an angle near your perspective, you would still observe bright spots). This is accounted for by taking the cosine angle formed (aka the dot product) of the surface normal and the “halfway vector” (the average of the light ray and camera ray) and exponentiating it with a high power, and multiplying the result by the radially-compensated light ray intensity.
